Chapter 14. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Overview

The fundamental theorem of calculus links the concept of differentiating a function with the concept of integrating a function. The first part of the theorem states that one of the antiderivatives, say , of some function may be obtained as the integral of with a variable bound of integration. This implies the existence of antiderivatives for continuous functions. Conversely, the second part of the theorem states that the integral of a function over some interval can be computed using any one, say , of its indefinite many antiderivatives. This part of the theorem has key practical applications, because explicitly finding the antiderivative of a function by symbolic integration allows for avoiding numerical integration to compute integrals.
